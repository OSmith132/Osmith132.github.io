Dissertation Project

Radiology has become an indispensable tool in modern healthcare, utilised across every sector of the medical field - from diagnosing broken bones to detecting cancer. The increasing reliance on radiology has led to a surge in the volume of radiological data, which continues to grow at a vastly disproportionate rate compared to the number of trained radiologists available to interpret it. As the demand for these services continues to rise, access to medical imaging remains limited in many regions, with a significant percentage of the global population having little or no access to diagnostic tools.


This growing demand has placed significant strain on healthcare providers worldwide, who are often forced to compensate by pushing for increased productivity, drastically raising workloads. In some cases, radiologists may be expected to interpret an image every few seconds during a full workday. Such unrealistic expectations inevitably heighten the risk of human error, potentially overlooking critical information in highly sensitive cases.


These challenges have been a driving force behind the development of artificial intelligence (AI) in the medical field, with substantial efforts focused on advancing AI-powered imaging. Integrating AI into the radiology workflow can significantly improve efficiency and reduce the level of manual input needed for interpreting medical images. This helps alleviate pressure on radiologists, allowing them to concentrate on more complex cases, while also improving both diagnostic accuracy and patient outcomes. In addition, AI-based tools can expand access to radiological imaging worldwide, enabling more reliable remote diagnostics and reducing dependency on highly trained specialists.


This project addresses these challenges by developing a semantic segmentation model that identifies and categorises dental structures such as lesions, fillings, and implants from 2D dental radiographs. By automating the analysis of dental images, the project demonstrates how AI and machine learning can enhance diagnostic efficiency, providing dental professionals with a reliable tool to streamline the imaging process and alleviate workload pressures.


Initially, the project explored a modified U-Net architecture, which has proven effective in many areas of medical image analysis. However, with the rise of transfer learning and large pre-trained foundation models, the research shifted towards adapting general segmentation models such as SAM and MedSAM. These were fine-tuned with domain-specific adaptations to enhance performance for dental imaging tasks, supported by techniques such as data augmentation and cross-validation to improve robustness and generalisation.


The research evaluates whether transfer learning is an effective approach for developing a model that generalises across varied clinical environments while maintaining high accuracy. Beyond improving dental diagnostics, the project also highlights the potential for expanding access to dental radiography interpretation in remote regions with limited availability of trained professionals. Ultimately, the project demonstrates the transformative potential of AI and deep learning in addressing the demand for dental image analysis and reducing reliance on limited human resources.
